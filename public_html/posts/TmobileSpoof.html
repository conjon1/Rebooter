What I did

Step one: Make File

sudo vim /etc/sysctl.d/99-hotspot.conf
  • Then inside the file
net.ipv4.ip_default_ttl=65
net.ipv6.conf.all.hop_limit=65

sudo sysctl --system OR reboot

NOTE: i had to disable ipv6 the tower could tell that it was not moblie data traffic

nmcli connection modify "Wired connection 2" ipv6.method "disabled"
nmcli connection up "Wired connection 2"

Step Two: Phone in USB Tether mode

There are two reasons for this

  • 1: More stable connection
  • 2: some carriers have a specific APNs that trigger by just turning on the hotspot, USB tethering circumvents that alltogether and you dont need to turn on the hotspot

NOTE: When put into tethering, Android creates a virtual ethernet interface on the computer. This is distinct from the phone's internal rmnet mobile data interface, but it bypasses standard Wi-Fi hotspot routing.

Step Three: VPN or proxy

Turn on your VPN or Proxy of choice inside your browser/computer
NOT THE PHONE
if you run the VPN on your phone and not your computer:

  • Your phone's data is encrypted.
  • Your tethered laptop's data bypasses the encryption entirely and goes naked to the carrier.

which is pretty wild but makes sense
This is to get around any DPI techniques that the carrier uses
(can also just run a vpn on both)

Why This Works

The TTL "Hop" Logic

  • Every router the data passes through subtracts 1 from the TTL (Time To Live) count
  • Phone default TTL: 64
  • Laptop default TTL: 64
Origin Start TTL Phone Hop (-1) Carrier Sees Result
Phone 64 N/A 64 Safe
Laptop 64 64 - 1 63 Throttled
Spoofed 65 65 - 1 64 Safe

Why the VPN matters

  • TTL tricks the counter, VPN tricks the "inspector"
  • Carrier uses DPI (Deep Packet Inspection) to check packet headers for desktop signatures (e.g. "Arch Linux" or "Mozilla/5.0 X11")
  • VPN wraps the data in a locked box
  • Carrier sees: Correct TTL (64) + Encrypted Garbage = Assumes it's just a secure phone app

NOTE: most cell providers don't do DPI unless something trips them up like using an excess of data
